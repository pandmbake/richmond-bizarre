Richmond Bizarre
Richmond, VA is a beautiful city. Underneath that beauty lies dark history with eerie locales that will provide your spine with an unlimited supply of shivers.
On this virtual tour, we'll stop at a few of the more infamous spots and discuss the grisly details that await. If you're ready, proceed on with caution as we take a stroll through one of Richmond's most beautiful, iconic, and sadly disturbing locations:
Hollywood Cemetery

High above the banks of the James River sits Hollywood Cemetery, a scrawling and hauntingly beautiful burial ground on 135 acres. In its hallowed ground lie 18,000 Confederate soldiers, 11,000 of whom are unknown. The cemetery is also home to James Monroe and John Tyler (both were US persidents) and Jefferson Davis, the president of the Confederacy during the Civil War. This is also the burial place of 22 Confederate generals, two Supreme Court Justices, and six Virginia governors.

Taking its name from the large number of holly trees growing throught the grounds, Hollywood Cemetery is a beautiful natural haven and a place of peace and reflextion with breathtaking views over the James river.

But let's not get carried away with beauty and splendor, for this sacred space is also home to eerie tales and several local legends that are guaranteed to creep under your skin.

Pictured on the left is the tomb of W.W. Pool, which is actually quite unremarkable in a cemetery filled with similar tombs. What makes this one special is what was seen disappearing into the tomb...

The date was October 2nd, 1925, and C&O locomotive #231 was passing through the Church Hill Tunnel, driven by engineer Tom Mason. The tunnel had been plaugued with various problems and a previous cave-in due to the soft clay soil, so there were workers constantly inside. Tom Mason's train that day consisted of 10 flatcars, and as he began to move the locomtive from inside the tunnel, a few bricks from overhead fell down on to the tracks. Soon, the whole tunnel began to collapse, with 100 feet of debris crashing down on Mason and many of the workmen who became trapped inside.

Pictured below-right is the sealed tunnel as it appears today in Church Hill.

Shortly after the disaster, a man emerged from the tunnel:

“Covered in blood, with jagged teeth visible and with flesh hanging from its body, this figure, barely recognizable as a human person, raced toward the James River...people tried to chase after the figure but it quickly took refuge in and disappeared into Hollywood Cemetery. It disappeared at the point where a mausoleum is located on the cemetery grounds. The creature vanished where the mausoleum is built into the hillside of Hollywood Cemetery. The name displayed in that location is that of W.W. Pool.”

And thus was created the urban legend of The Richmond Vampire.

Now, let us move on, shall we, just uphill from Mr. Pool's resting place to the grave of little Florence Rees.

Florence died in 1862 at the age of two from Scarlett Fever, a sadly common ailment in the 19th century. Her unusual gravesite is pictured below, complete with a life-sized, black, cast-iron Newfoundland dog statue standing guard.

One explaination for the statue is found in the story of an anonymous storekeeper who gave the dog to Mr. and Mrs. Rees in honor of Florence, because she admired the dog at his shop on Main Street. Those who get close to the statue sometimes report hearing random barking, and those who lean in a little too closely report hearing disembodied growling. If that isn't creepy enough, some folks have reported seeing the dog facing the opposite direction, perhaps to protect little Florence beneath.

Hollywood Cemetery is a MUST SEE stop on any tour of Richmond, and is a great place for locals to experience some of the best views of the city. The gates are open to visitors 7 days a week, from 8:00 am to 5:00 pm (6:00 pm during Daylight Savings Time). You can walk, drive, or even set up a Segway tour by visiting the cemetery's website. The cemetery is especially beautiful in the autumn, when ghosts and vampires come out to play.

Now, let's proceed just two miles northwest to one of Richmond's most hip shopping areas to visit the iconic Byrd Theater.
